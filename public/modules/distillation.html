Knowledge Distillation

Teacher → student, soft-label KL objective, and when to distill vs quantize.

Start Here

Let's start with a small classroom analogy, because it gets to the whole idea. Imagine two ways of teaching a student. In the first, you hand back every quiz marked only right or wrong — no explanation, just the correct answer. In the second, an experienced teacher sits beside the student and says, 'Well, the answer is billing, but honestly this one looks a lot like an account issue, and it's nothing like spam.' Which student learns faster? Obviously the second — because they're absorbing not just the answer, but the teacher's whole sense of how the categories relate.

Now here's the puzzle we'll chase. When we train a small model, we almost always teach it the first way: a labeled dataset gives it right-or-wrong answers and nothing more. And a small model trained like that tends to plateau below a big one — we usually blame its size. But what if part of the gap isn't size at all? What if it's that we've been giving it the impoverished kind of feedback, when a big model already holds the rich, teacherly version?

Take your time with this. We'll pin down exactly what information a plain label throws away, watch how a large model's fuller answer smuggles that information back in, and see the precise trick — turning up a 'temperature' knob — that makes the hidden signal loud enough to learn from. By the end you'll understand why a well-distilled small model beats an identical one trained from scratch.

How It Works

Start from what a labeled dataset actually gives a small model. A hard label is one-hot: for an email that is really 'billing', the target is [billing=1, technical=0, account=0, spam=0, ...]. Cross-entropy against that one-hot target tells the model exactly one thing — the correct class — and asserts that every other class is equally and maximally wrong.

But that's false. A billing dispute is much closer to an account-access complaint than to spam. The one-hot target throws that similarity structure away.

A small model trained from scratch has to rediscover which classes are near which others purely from co-occurrence in its limited data, and with limited capacity it often can't. That is why the from-scratch small model plateaus below the big one: not only fewer parameters, but a poorer training signal.

Knowledge distillation replaces (or blends) the one-hot target with the teacher's full output distribution — the probabilities the large model assigns across ALL classes/tokens. This is the core idea. Instead of 'the answer is billing,' the target becomes the teacher's soft judgment: 'billing 0.72, account 0.21, technical 0.06, spam 0.001, ...'.

Geoff Hinton called the information hiding in those small non-top probabilities dark knowledge. The teacher saying account=0.21 (not 0.001) tells the student that billing and account are confusable neighbors; the teacher saying spam≈0.001 tells the student spam is nowhere near.

A hard label carries roughly log2(num_classes) bits about this example; the soft distribution carries a graded similarity signal over every class at once. The student learns the teacher's geometry of the problem, not just its verdict.

The objective. Distillation trains the student to match the teacher's distribution, measured by KL divergence — the same quantity from the training-signal module, the extra bits you pay to encode samples from p (teacher) using the student's code q. Minimizing KL(teacher‖student) over the soft targets pulls the student's whole distribution toward the teacher's, including the ranking of the non-top classes.

In practice you blend two terms: L = α · T² · KL(soft_teacher ‖ soft_student) + (1−α) · CE(hard_label, student). The hard-label cross-entropy keeps the student anchored to ground truth where labels exist; the KL term transfers the dark knowledge. A typical α is 0.5–0.9 — the soft signal usually does most of the work.

Temperature exposes the dark knowledge (and the T² fix)
Teacher logits z for one example (4 classes):  [4.0, 3.0, -1.0, -3.0]

Softmax at T=1 (softmax(z)):
  billing 0.708 | account 0.260 | technical 0.005 | spam 0.0006
  -> account 0.26 is visible but technical/spam are ~0: the near-neighbor
     signal is faint, and gradients from those tiny probs are negligible.

Softmax at T=3 (softmax(z / T)):
  billing 0.452 | account 0.324 | technical 0.121 | spam 0.062
  -> the RELATIVE structure is now exposed: billing > account > technical > spam.
     "spam is far, technical is closer, account is a real neighbor" is now
     a strong training signal the student can actually fit.

Why T raises T=1 -> T=3? Softening flattens the distribution so the ratios
between small classes carry gradient. You apply the SAME temperature T to the
student's logits during training, then softmax both.

The T^2 detail: soft targets from softmax(z/T) shrink each logit's gradient by
~1/T^2 (a 1/T from the softened student output, a 1/T from the softened target).
Multiplying the distillation loss by T^2 rescales it back so the soft-target
gradients stay comparable in magnitude to the hard-label CE gradients when you
blend them. Drop the T^2 and the soft term is silently down-weighted ~9x at T=3.

Temperature, precisely. At inference T=1 you want a peaked distribution. But for transfer you deliberately raise the temperature (T>1) on BOTH teacher and student logits before the softmax. A higher T flattens both distributions, magnifying the relative probabilities of the non-top classes — exactly the dark knowledge you want to move.

Without softening, technical=0.005 and spam=0.0006 are numerically tiny and produce almost no gradient, so the neighbor structure never transfers; the student effectively falls back to learning near-hard labels.

The catch is that softening by T shrinks the gradient magnitude of the soft term by ~1/T², so the standard recipe multiplies the distillation loss by T² to keep it on the same scale as the hard-label term — otherwise the soft signal gets silently down-weighted and you've thrown away the thing you turned temperature up to capture.

Variants, at a naming level.

(1) Response-based / logit distillation: match the teacher's output distribution — everything above. Simplest, most general, and all you need if you only have the teacher's outputs.

(2) Feature-based: also match intermediate representations — the student is trained so its hidden layers mirror the teacher's (this is how DistilBERT hits ~97% of BERT's GLUE score at ~40% fewer parameters). Needs white-box access and a projection to align dimensions.

(3) Relation-based: match relationships BETWEEN examples (e.g. pairwise distances in representation space) rather than per-example outputs.

(4) Sequence-level distillation for autoregressive LLMs: matching per-token distributions is weak because errors compound over a generation; instead you train the student on the teacher's actual generated sequences (often the teacher's beam/greedy output), so the student learns to reproduce whole trajectories the teacher would produce.

The modern LLM pattern is (4) taken to its logical end: use a strong teacher to GENERATE the training data. You don't need the teacher's logits at all — you sample outputs, or richer, ask the teacher for step-by-step rationales, and fine-tune a smaller model on that synthetic corpus.

Alpaca distilled instruction-following from a stronger model's generations; Orca went further by training on the teacher's reasoning traces, not just final answers; the Phi and Gemma lines lean heavily on high-quality (often teacher-generated / filtered) data to punch far above their parameter count.

Mechanistically this is still distillation — you're transferring the teacher's behavior through its outputs — but it works black-box, over any API, with no access to logits or weights. The soft-label/KL machinery and the synthetic-data machinery are the same idea (copy the teacher's output distribution) at two different levels of access.

The decision: distill vs quantize vs prune vs just use a smaller base. These attack different axes.

Quantization keeps the SAME architecture and shrinks the numeric precision of the weights (fp16 -> int8/int4) — cheap, no training run, but it only takes you so small before quality falls off a cliff, and it can't change the model's capability. Pruning removes weights/heads/channels, usually needing a fine-tune to recover.

Distillation is the expensive-but-powerful option: it requires a training run and access to a teacher (logits OR just generations), but it can produce a genuinely smaller architecture that retains quality a same-size model trained from scratch cannot reach — because it inherits the teacher's learned similarity structure.

Crucially they COMPOSE and are usually stacked: distill the 70B down to an 8B student to recover most of the quality, THEN quantize that 8B to int4 for deployment. Distillation buys quality-per-parameter; quantization buys bytes-per-parameter.

Limits, stated plainly so you don't oversell it.

First, the student rarely exceeds the teacher on the distilled distribution — you are copying the teacher's judgments, including its mistakes and biases; distillation transfers a ceiling, it doesn't raise one. (Self-distillation and ensemble-teacher tricks nibble at this but don't overturn it.)

Second, coverage bounds transfer: the student only learns the teacher's behavior on the inputs you actually distill over. If your distillation data doesn't cover a slice of the input space, the student has no signal there and will diverge from the teacher exactly where you didn't look — a narrow synthetic set gives a narrow student.

Third, capacity gaps are real: if the student is far too small relative to the task, no amount of soft labels closes the gap — it will plateau below the teacher no matter how good the signal, because it lacks the representational room to hold the teacher's function.

When a student plateaus well below its teacher, the first two suspects are always (a) too little capacity or (b) distillation data that doesn't cover the failing region — not the temperature or α.

Key Points

  • A one-hot hard label throws away similarity structure. It asserts every wrong class is equally wrong, so a from-scratch small model must rediscover which classes are near-neighbors from scratch — a poorer signal, not just fewer parameters.
  • Distillation trains the student on the teacher's full soft distribution. The small non-top probabilities are 'dark knowledge' — account=0.21 vs spam=0.001 tells the student which classes are confusable. The student learns the teacher's geometry, not just its verdict.
  • Objective blends KL to the teacher + hard-label CE: `L = α·T²·KL(soft_teacher‖soft_student) + (1−α)·CE`. KL transfers dark knowledge; CE anchors to ground truth (α typically 0.5–0.9).
  • Temperature T>1 flattens both distributions to expose near-zero-class gradients; softening shrinks the soft gradient ~1/T², so multiply the distillation loss by T² to keep it comparable to the hard-label term.
  • Modern LLM distillation = train on the teacher's generated data/rationales (Alpaca, Orca, Phi, Gemma). Same idea as soft labels — copy the teacher's output distribution — but black-box, no logits needed.
  • Distillation composes with quantization: distill 70B→8B for quality-per-parameter, then quantize to int4 for bytes-per-parameter. Limits: student can't exceed the teacher, transfers only what the data covers, and plateaus if too small.

In Production — Apply It

Now let's put all of that to work on a real one. A 70B model hits the quality bar on a customer-support classification and drafting task, but it costs $8 per million tokens and adds 900ms of latency the team can't ship. Product needs something ~10× cheaper and faster that keeps most of the quality. A smaller base model trained from scratch on the same labeled data plateaus 6 points below the 70B.

Take a moment before reading on — why would training the small model on the teacher's outputs beat training it directly on the very same labeled data? Here's the reasoning, step by step. The from-scratch model was fed one-hot labels, which assert every wrong class is equally wrong and throw away all the similarity structure — so part of that 6-point gap is a poorer signal, not just fewer parameters. Distillation swaps the target for the 70B's full softened distribution: 'billing 0.72, account 0.21, spam 0.001.' That 0.21-vs-0.001 gap — the dark knowledge — hands the student the teacher's geometry of the problem for free, exactly what a one-hot label couldn't express. Notice how directly this addresses the plateau: the student inherits learned structure it could never have rediscovered from its limited data, so it recovers most of the gap a same-size from-scratch model can't. And the deployment move follows straight from the earlier decision: distill 70B → 8B for quality-per-parameter, then quantize that 8B to int4 for bytes-per-parameter — the ~10× cost and latency cut, quality largely intact.

Key Insight

Distillation trains a small student to copy a large teacher's full output distribution, not its argmax — the non-top probabilities ('dark knowledge') carry similarity structure a one-hot label throws away, which is why a distilled student beats a same-size model trained from scratch. Blend KL to the teacher's temperature-softened distribution (with the T² rescale) plus hard-label CE; at the LLM level this generalizes to fine-tuning on the teacher's generated data/rationales. Distillation buys quality-per-parameter and composes with quantization (distill then quantize) — but the student can't exceed the teacher, transfers only what the distillation data covers, and plateaus if it's simply too small.
